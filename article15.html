The Problem: When Antibiotics Make Things Worse

Antibiotics are one of modern medicine's most powerful tools — and one of its most dangerous double-edged swords. Every course of antibiotics doesn't just kill the target pathogen. It also disrupts the gut microbiome: the trillions of bacteria that colonize the human digestive tract and regulate everything from immune function to neurotransmitter production. In many patients, this disruption is temporary. In some, it creates an opening for something far more dangerous.

Clostridioides difficile — C. difficile, or CDI — is a bacterial pathogen that causes severe colitis and is responsible for roughly 500,000 infections and 30,000 deaths annually in the United States alone. CDI is, in a brutal irony, primarily an antibiotic-associated disease: it thrives in the disrupted gut environment left behind by broad-spectrum antibiotics. Standard treatment is yet more antibiotics — but recurrence rates are high, and some patients develop multi-drug-resistant CDI that cannot be treated with any available antibiotic.

This is where fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) — transferring healthy gut bacteria from a screened donor into a patient — has emerged as a breakthrough intervention. FMT success rates in recurrent CDI consistently exceed 80–90% in clinical studies, far outperforming any antibiotic regimen. The FDA approved the first commercial FMT product (Rebyota) in late 2022. But in Korea, the pharmaceutical and regulatory infrastructure to manufacture, bank, and supply FMT has been almost entirely absent — until one company decided to build it.

"The gut microbiome is the key to the treatment of diseases that modern medicine cannot yet cure. Our goal is to make microbiome-based medicine practical — starting with the world's most reliable collection of healthy donor bacteria."
— Lee Dong-ho (이동호), CEO, BioBank Healing

The Founder: Korea's Leading Gut Microbiome Scientist Turns Entrepreneur

Lee Dong-ho (이동호) did not arrive at microbiome therapeutics as an outsider. He spent his career as a gastroenterologist and researcher at Seoul National University College of Medicine (서울대학교 의과대학) — Korea's most prestigious medical institution — where he became one of the country's foremost experts on gut microbiome research and was eventually named an Emeritus Professor (명예교수). During his academic career, he was among the first Korean physicians to conduct clinical FMT procedures and to study the therapeutic mechanisms of gut microbiota transfer.

That clinical foundation is critical context. Most microbiome startups are built by scientists working at the interface of biology and drug chemistry — but without firsthand experience performing FMT on real patients, understanding the donor screening protocols that determine product quality, or managing the cold-chain and manufacturing constraints that make FMT a uniquely difficult product to commercialize. Lee Dong-ho entered BioBank Healing with all three.

In October 2016, Lee founded BioBank Healing in Seongnam, Gyeonggi Province — close to Bundang Seoul National University Hospital, which became the company's anchor clinical partner. The founding premise was specific: Korea had no stool bank, no FMT manufacturing infrastructure, and no regulatory framework for FMT as a pharmaceutical product. BioBank Healing would build all three simultaneously, using the clinical relationships Lee had spent decades cultivating as its primary competitive moat.

The Science: What FMT Actually Does — and Why It's Hard to Make

Fecal microbiota transplantation is, at its simplest, the transfer of processed stool from a healthy, thoroughly screened donor to a patient whose gut microbiome has been disrupted. The mechanism of action — unlike conventional drugs — is not a single molecule acting on a single target. FMT introduces a complex ecosystem of thousands of bacterial species, each interacting with the host's immune system, mucosal lining, and existing microbiota. The therapeutic effect is the restoration of a healthy, diverse microbial community.

That complexity makes FMT extraordinarily difficult to standardize. The challenge is not the transplantation procedure itself — which can be delivered colonoscopically, via nasogastric tube, or in capsule form. The challenge is the product: ensuring that donor stool is collected from individuals with genuinely healthy microbiomes, processed using techniques that preserve microbial viability, tested for pathogens that could harm immunocompromised recipients, and stored in ways that maintain product integrity across delivery to multiple hospital sites.

This is what a stool bank (대변은행) does. BioBank Healing built Asia's first and Korea's largest stool bank, establishing donor screening protocols, processing and cryopreservation infrastructure, and quality assurance systems that meet the standards required for hospital procurement. The result is a manufacturing operation capable of supplying standardized FMT product at consistent quality to hospitals across Korea — a capability that did not exist in the country before BioBank Healing built it.

The company has also developed a next-generation probiotics platform alongside its FMT manufacturing business: using its unique repository of characterized gut microbial strains to identify and culture specific bacterial species that drive FMT's therapeutic effects, with the goal of eventually producing defined-composition products (rather than full-stool transplants) with more predictable and regulatable pharmacology.

The Pipeline: From Hospital Supplier to Drug Developer

BioBank Healing's immediate revenue comes from its FMT supply business — a B2B model that sells processed FMT product to hospitals performing transplantation procedures. With 10+ hospital partners and ~90% clinical success rates in CDI, this business has established the company as Korea's dominant FMT supplier and generated the clinical data that underpins the drug development program.

Beyond CDI and antibiotic resistance, BioBank Healing is pursuing extended indications that reflect the emerging scientific evidence linking gut microbiome composition to systemic disease. The company has disclosed research directions in dementia (the gut-brain axis is one of the most active areas of microbiome science), autism spectrum disorder (multiple clinical trials have shown microbiome interventions affecting behavioral markers), and immuno-oncology (gut microbiome composition is now established as a predictor of checkpoint inhibitor response in solid tumor patients).

The immuno-oncology direction is particularly strategically interesting. Multiple Phase III trials — and the clinical practices of oncology centers at Memorial Sloan Kettering, MD Anderson, and several European cancer institutes — have shown that patients with high gut microbiome diversity respond significantly better to anti-PD-1 therapy than patients with disrupted microbiomes. This has created a window for FMT-based microbiome restoration as an adjunct to checkpoint inhibitor therapy — a combination that could dramatically expand both the addressable patient population and the pricing power of microbiome therapeutics.

Strategic Partnership: Samyang Foods and Sarcopenia

In an indication of how seriously Korean corporates are engaging with the microbiome space, BioBank Healing has been in discussions with Samyang Foods (삼양식품) — the food and health products conglomerate best known internationally for its Buldak (fire chicken) noodles — on a joint research agreement covering sarcopenia (근감소증, age-related muscle loss) microbiome therapy and related health functional food development.

The scientific basis: research published in 2024–2025 established clear associations between specific gut bacterial strains (particularly Lacticaseibacillus and Faecalibacterium) and muscle health markers, with FMT interventions demonstrating measurable effects on age-related muscle disorder in animal models. For Samyang, which has been building a health and functional food portfolio alongside its consumer food business, microbiome-based muscle health represents a large-TAM product opportunity. For BioBank Healing, the partnership opens a commercialization pathway for microbiome research into the consumer health segment — a much shorter route to revenue than full pharmaceutical development.

The Market: $2B (₩2.7조) and Expanding Fast

The global FMT market was valued at approximately $2 billion (₩2.7조) in 2023 and is projected to grow at a double-digit CAGR through the end of the decade, driven by FDA approval of commercial FMT products, expanding clinical indications, and growing recognition of the gut microbiome's role in systemic disease. The CDI indication — BioBank Healing's primary commercial focus — represents the most developed and largest immediate segment, but the long-term opportunity is far broader.

The broader microbiome therapeutics market — encompassing defined bacterial consortia products, FMT-derived drugs, and microbiome-modulating small molecules — is projected to reach $9–12 billion ($12–16조) by 2030 as the science matures and regulatory frameworks for live biotherapeutic products (LBPs) solidify globally. Korea's domestic market, while smaller, benefits from a healthcare system (NHIS) with strong coverage of advanced therapeutics and a hospital network that is already accustomed to FMT as a clinical procedure through BioBank Healing's supply infrastructure.

Korea's regulatory environment for FMT is evolving. The Ministry of Food and Drug Safety (MFDS / 식약처) has been developing LBP regulatory guidelines consistent with international standards, a development that BioBank Healing — as the market's established infrastructure provider — is positioned to benefit from as clinical products move toward formal IND filing and approval pathways.

Competitive Landscape

BioBank Healing operates in a global microbiome therapeutics space that has attracted significant capital and several well-funded players — but its Korea-specific positioning and manufacturing infrastructure give it meaningful local advantages:

Global FMT Drug Leaders: Ferring Pharmaceuticals (Rebyota — FDA-approved 2022) and Seres Therapeutics (Vowst — FDA-approved 2023) have established the first commercial LBP products for CDI in the US market. Both are defined-composition products derived from donor stool, rather than full FMT — similar to the direction BioBank Healing's BBH-MS01 is heading. These companies have validated the regulatory pathway and commercial market, but are not directly competing in Korea's hospital FMT supply market, where BioBank Healing is the dominant incumbent.

Korean Competition: The microbiome therapeutics space in Korea is nascent. Issue #008 covered SMT Bio (에스엠티바이오), another Korean microbiome company — but SMT Bio's focus is on defined probiotic formulations for different indications, rather than stool banking and FMT supply. The two companies occupy different parts of the value chain.

International Stool Banks: OpenBiome (US) was the world's leading stool bank before transitioning to a research model; Rebiotix (acquired by Ferring) and other players have moved toward manufactured products rather than raw FMT supply. BioBank Healing's Asia-first positioning means it faces no direct stool banking competition in Korea's hospital market — a structural advantage that has compounded over nearly a decade of operational history.

Signal Box — Why BioBank Healing Matters
  • The stool bank is the moat: A decade of donor recruitment, screening, and characterization cannot be replicated quickly. BioBank Healing's repository of characterized microbial strains is the foundational asset from which every future product will be derived — and it has a ~10-year head start on any potential Korean competitor attempting to build equivalent infrastructure.
  • Hospital relationships = distribution: The 10+ hospital FMT supply contracts are not just revenue — they are clinical data pipelines, regulatory endorsements, and distribution channels for future pharmaceutical products. Every hospital that currently uses BioBank Healing's FMT supply is a potential launch customer for BBH-MS01.
  • Founder credibility is clinical, not academic: Lee Dong-ho is not a laboratory scientist who pivoted to entrepreneurship. He is one of Korea's most respected gastroenterologists, with decades of clinical FMT experience and relationships with the hospital medical directors who make procurement decisions. That is a meaningful advantage over biotech founders who need to build clinical credibility from scratch.
  • FDA/MFDS pathway is now cleared: The approval of Rebyota and Vowst in the US has de-risked the regulatory story for microbiome LBPs globally. Korean MFDS is developing a parallel framework. BioBank Healing's drug development program now has regulatory precedent to follow — a critical advantage that didn't exist when the company was founded.
  • Immuno-oncology adjacency is underappreciated: The gut microbiome-checkpoint inhibitor connection is one of the most exciting developments in oncology. If BioBank Healing can demonstrate that FMT-based microbiome restoration improves checkpoint inhibitor response rates, the addressable market expands dramatically — into combination therapy protocols at cancer hospitals that already partner with the company for CDI treatment.
  • The Samyang partnership signals corporate demand: When a major food company with a $5B+ market cap starts a joint R&D program with a microbiome startup, it signals that the science has matured enough to attract non-pharma capital and distribution networks. Samyang's involvement could provide BioBank Healing with a consumer health revenue stream that partially de-risks the longer pharma timeline.

Investment Thesis

BioBank Healing sits at the intersection of two inflection points: a microbiome therapeutics sector that is transitioning from scientific curiosity to approved pharmaceutical products, and a Korean healthcare market where the company has a decade of operational and clinical infrastructure that no competitor can replicate quickly.

The risks are real. FMT drug development is early-stage by global pharmaceutical standards — BBH-MS01 has not yet entered clinical trials, and the path from preclinical data to commercial approval in Korea (and potentially globally) is measured in years, not months. The company's Pre-A capital base is modest relative to the capital requirements of a full pharmaceutical development program, and a Series A round that funds IND-enabling studies and a Phase I trial will be the company's most important near-term milestone. Investor appetite for microbiome therapeutics in Korea — while growing — has historically been cautious relative to oncology, where clinical biomarkers and regulatory pathways are more established.

What makes BioBank Healing worth tracking against these risks is a structural positioning that most biotech startups cannot build: the stool bank creates a defensible, cash-generating asset that funds the drug development program while simultaneously generating the clinical data and regulatory relationships that the drug program needs. The company is not burning cash on a single clinical bet — it has an operating business that validates the therapeutic approach daily across 10+ hospitals.

The milestones to watch: IND filing for BBH-MS01 in Korea, the structure and size of the Series A round, the specifics of any Samyang partnership announcement, and whether BioBank Healing can position itself as the microbiome supply infrastructure partner for Korean immuno-oncology programs — a role that could transform its strategic relevance from a CDI specialist to a platform company at the center of Korea's most active cancer therapeutic sector.

Asteromorph (애스터로모프)
Seed · $31.4M (₩420억)
Building AI that can conduct scientific research autonomously — and Korea's government is betting on it

Asteromorph closed a ₩420억 (~$31.4M) seed round in May 2026 — one of the largest seed rounds in Korean startup history — to build what the company describes as "scientific superintelligence": AI systems capable of autonomously conducting the full scientific research cycle, from hypothesis generation through experimental design and data interpretation. Lead investor Bon Angels was joined by Future Play, Mirae Asset Venture Investment, IMM Investment, Korea Development Bank, STIC Ventures, and SV Investment, among others.

The strategic significance extends beyond the funding amount. CEO Lee Min-hyung was subsequently appointed as the project director for the AI scientist mission of K-Moonshot — the South Korean government's flagship national AI research initiative. That appointment effectively makes Asteromorph the private-sector anchor of Korea's bet that AI-driven scientific discovery can become a national capability rather than a capability that Korea imports from US and European labs.

Signal: A ₩420억 seed with KDB participation and K-Moonshot project leadership is an unusual convergence of private and public capital on a single company. The scientific superintelligence thesis — AI that can run the research loop, not just assist researchers — is either the next major productivity technology or premature. The combination of that capital base and government mandate means Asteromorph will generate definitive evidence one way or another within 2–3 years.
Galux (갤럭스)
Series B · $29M (₩420억)
De novo AI antibody design — building a drug discovery platform that starts from scratch instead of screening libraries

Galux closed a ₩420억 ($29M) Series B in February 2026, bringing total funding to ₩680억 ($46M). The company's platform — GaluxDesign — uses AI to design protein structures de novo: rather than screening millions of existing compounds for desired drug properties, Galux starts with a target protein and designs new molecules from first principles to have the desired therapeutic function. The Series B included Korea Investment & Securities as co-lead (an IPO underwriter signal), with Yuanta Investment, Korea Development Bank, Mirae Asset Securities, STIC Ventures, and LigaChem BioSciences (strategic investor) also participating.

The strategic investor angle is notable: LigaChem BioSciences is one of Korea's leading ADC (antibody-drug conjugate) companies, with multiple global pharma co-development and licensing deals. LigaChem's involvement as both a co-development partner and investor in Galux's Series B suggests that the GaluxDesign platform has generated antibody candidates of sufficient quality to attract a partner with rigorous commercial standards. The presence of Korea Investment & Securities as IPO underwriter signals active KOSDAQ listing preparation.

Signal: De novo protein design is the frontier of AI drug discovery — AlphaFold's impact on structure prediction was the proof-of-concept; Galux is betting that the same deep learning advances can drive therapeutic design from first principles. The LigaChem strategic investment and IPO preparation suggest the clinical pipeline is maturing faster than the funding timeline alone implies. Watch for technology transfer or out-licensing announcements in H2 2026.
BreezeBio (formerly GenEdit)
Series B · $60M (₩800억)
Precision genetic medicine with organ-targeting delivery — taking immune tolerance for Type 1 diabetes into IND-enabling studies

BreezeBio (formerly GenEdit) announced a $60M (~₩800억) Series B in February 2026 to advance its NanoGalaxy organ-targeting delivery platform and its lead program BRZ-101 — a genetic medicine designed to restore immune tolerance in Type 1 diabetes. The NanoGalaxy platform delivers mRNA encoding autoantigens and tolerogenic co-factors directly to antigen-presenting cells, with the goal of inducing antigen-specific regulatory T cells that block the autoimmune response driving T1D — without broadly suppressing the immune system.

Preclinical data showed antigen-specific immune tolerance induction in autoimmune disease models including a mouse model of diabetes, with good tolerability in mice and nonhuman primates. The Series B, led by Yuanta Investment and DSC Investment with participation from STIC Ventures, Kiwoom Investment, Korea Investment Partners, KDB Silicon Valley, and ACVC Partners, funds IND-enabling studies targeting a clinical trial initiation. The company's rebrand from GenEdit to BreezeBio signals a deliberate positioning shift from gene editing toward the broader precision genetic medicine category.

Signal: Type 1 diabetes immune tolerance is one of immunology's most ambitious targets — if you can shut down the autoimmune attack without global immunosuppression, the therapeutic and commercial implications are enormous. BreezeBio's delivery platform (NanoGalaxy) is the asset to watch — organ-targeting mRNA delivery is the capability that could extend this technology beyond T1D into a broad range of autoimmune and tolerance-related diseases. IND filing timeline and preclinical data publication are the next key milestones.
