"While designing chips for microsecond-level high-frequency trading at Morgan Stanley, I realized the cost structure of AI infrastructure was fundamentally broken. And I knew Korea had the best memory partners in the world — Samsung and SK Hynix."

After designing Starlink satellite ASICs at SpaceX and building high-frequency trading chips on Wall Street, Park Sung-hyun made an unusual bet: he moved back to Korea to start a semiconductor company in 2020. What sets his story apart is that he didn't return because he could build there — he returned because Korea was the only place where his specific strategy was viable. No country in the world can access cutting-edge HBM memory earlier or on better terms than Korea.

In September 2020, he co-founded Rebellions with Oh Jin-wook, Kim Hyo-eun, and Shin Sung-ho. Less than five years later, it became Korea's first AI semiconductor unicorn.

Technology deep dive

Why inference-only ASICs — and Nvidia's structural dilemma

The AI chip market breaks into two distinct phases. Training is about building the model. Inference is about running it in production. Nvidia's GPUs are general-purpose hardware optimized for training. The problem: the moment a company deploys AI into a live service, the majority of infrastructure costs come from inference — not training.

Inference is fundamentally a memory bandwidth problem. Every time an LLM generates a single token, it must load tens of billions of parameters from memory. The GPU's raw compute (TFLOPS) is underutilized — memory bandwidth is the bottleneck. Rebellions rebuilt the architecture from scratch around that reality.

REBEL-Quad: the world's first UCIe-Advanced AI accelerator

REBEL-Quad is a chiplet architecture connecting four NPU dies via UCIe-Advanced interconnects. Rather than a single monolithic chip, four dies operate as one unified processor. Each die carries a 36GB HBM3E stack, giving the package a total of 144GB of HBM3E memory. The die-to-die interconnect runs at 16Gbps with 4TB/s aggregate bandwidth and 11ns latency.

More important than raw performance is the 20.7% advantage in power efficiency (TFLOPS/W). In a world where power costs account for 30–40% of data center opex, delivering the same performance at 14% lower wattage translates directly into significant TCO savings at scale.

Why this company
Problem
Running ChatGPT for a single day consumes roughly 10x the electricity of a Google search. As AI services scale, inference infrastructure costs become a direct line item on the P&L. Nvidia's H100/H200 are architected for training — at the inference stage, a significant portion of their compute sits idle.
Tech edge
Inference workloads have a structural characteristic: they are memory bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound. REBEL-Quad addresses this through UCIe-Advanced die-to-die interconnects. The core innovation is making four NPU dies behave as a single processor, distributing memory access patterns transparently across all chiplets. The architecture was formally presented at ISSCC 2026.
Moat
① Memory supply chain: Partnership with Samsung and SK Hynix means priority access to HBM3E — a structural advantage that US fabless players simply cannot replicate from across the Pacific.

② ARM's first APAC startup investment: ARM chose Rebellions as the most compelling AI chip startup built on its architecture — bringing ecosystem access and joint go-to-market along with the capital.

③ Open-source integration: Official integrations with PyTorch, vLLM, and Red Hat AI reduce the friction for developers switching away from Nvidia's CUDA stack.
Global demand
The AI inference market is projected to grow 12x from $39B in 2024 to $475B by 2030. Rebellions' strategic targets are AI-native companies like Meta and xAI that build their own infrastructure rather than relying on hyperscaler clouds. Hyperscalers build their own chips — but this middle tier of AI-first companies desperately needs an alternative to Nvidia. Entities established in the US, Japan, Saudi Arabia, and Taiwan.
vs. Competitors
vs. Nvidia: Inference-specialized ASIC vs. general-purpose GPU. Nvidia is structurally constrained from radically changing its architecture — too much depends on preserving the CUDA ecosystem.

vs. Groq/Cerebras: US competitors have limited access to cutting-edge HBM. Geographic proximity to Samsung and SK Hynix is Rebellions' core cost advantage.

vs. Google TPU/AWS Trainium: Hyperscaler chips are locked to their own cloud. Rebellions offers cloud-agnostic hardware deployable on-premise or across any cloud environment.

Customer traction

Who is already buying — and why it matters

The most credible signal of product-market fit isn't a pitch deck — it's a paying customer. Rebellions' commercial roster covers three distinct buyer segments: strategic investors who deploy the chips internally, domestic hyperscalers building national AI infrastructure, and the global chip architecture stack itself.

ARM

ARM Holdings

Strategic investor · Architecture partnership

Confirmed

ARM made Rebellions its first-ever startup investment in APAC — not a financial play, but a strategic endorsement. REBEL-Quad is built on ARM's Neoverse compute core, and the partnership gives Rebellions priority access to next-generation IP and the ARM ecosystem's global enterprise network. When the world's most widely deployed chip architecture backs you as its APAC flagship, the implied customer pipeline is significant.

KT

KT Corporation

Telecom · AI cloud deployment

Confirmed

Korea's second-largest telecom operator has deployed Rebellions' ATOM chip in its AI inference infrastructure. KT's enterprise AI cloud — offered to Korean corporates running LLM workloads — runs on REBEL hardware. This is a meaningful reference: telcos are among the most demanding buyers for inference-at-scale, with strict SLA requirements and procurement cycles that favor proven, in-production silicon over prototypes.

SK

SK Telecom

Telecom · AI inference pipeline

Pipeline

Korea's largest telecom operator and operator of the A.X Platform — one of Asia's most aggressive national LLM infrastructure programs — is evaluating REBEL-Quad for its domestic inference stack. SKT has publicly committed to building a sovereign AI compute layer independent of US chip supply chains. Rebellions is the only domestic ASIC vendor capable of fulfilling that mandate at scale.

삼성

Samsung Electronics (via Sapeon merger)

Strategic · Customer network access

Confirmed

In 2024, Rebellions absorbed Sapeon — Samsung's in-house AI chip spinoff — in a strategic merger. The deal was not a simple acqui-hire: it transferred Sapeon's existing Samsung enterprise customer relationships directly to Rebellions, along with its engineering team and chip IP. Samsung's sprawling semiconductor procurement network is now effectively a distribution channel for Rebellions' next-generation products.

Investor signal: Rebellions has cleared the most important early hardware hurdle — it has moved from chip samples to production deployments with Tier-1 buyers. The combination of ARM's architecture endorsement + telecom-scale production usage + Samsung's inherited customer base is a traction profile that most semiconductor startups take 8–10 years to assemble.
Risks
01

The CUDA switching cost

The global AI developer stack is built on Nvidia's CUDA. PyTorch and vLLM integrations lower the barrier, but enterprise migration cycles typically run 12–24 months. Developer ecosystem adoption is the real gating factor.

02

Hardware startup manufacturing risk

Chip design performance and mass production are entirely different challenges. RebelPOD and RebelRack are still in early-stage scale-up. Yield issues or supply chain disruptions could surface at the worst possible time — just ahead of the IPO.

03

Nvidia's inference-focused response

Nvidia has already dramatically improved inference efficiency with the Blackwell architecture. Whether Rebellions' energy efficiency advantage survives the next generation of Nvidia GPUs remains unconfirmed.

What to watch

2026 H2 IPO pricing band — How does the market value this against the pre-IPO $2.34B? A real-time read on AI semiconductor sector multiples.

Supply deal with Meta or xAI — These are Rebellions' stated primary targets. A single announced contract becomes a global reference that reshapes the entire sales cycle.

RebelPOD mass production schedule — This is when the gap between benchmark numbers and real-world data center performance becomes visible.

First US enterprise customer disclosure — The US entity is set up; what's missing is a visible sales pipeline. The first named customer changes the narrative.

Ones to Watch

Early-stage Korean startups worth putting on your radar — curated signals for global investors tracking Korea's next breakout companies.

Ones to watch

Xup

Field robotics  ·  LG Electronics spinout  ·  TIPS + DIPS dual designation

An unusual case: TIPS and DIPS designation within two months of spinning out of LG Electronics. The platform starts with golf course automation and has a clear expansion roadmap into agriculture and defense. Worth watching in the context of Bessemer's projection that the first $50B robotics company will come from defense. Early stage, but LG's network and dual government certification meaningfully de-risk the early innings.

Early Stage Robotics

ActionPower

Multimodal AI workflow  ·  Series B  ·  KRW 6B

A B2B platform that connects internal documents, meetings, and email through multimodal AI to automate enterprise workflows. Closed Series B on the back of major Korean conglomerate customers. The thesis: they're targeting the first integration layer where AI agents enter real enterprise environments — a globally scalable wedge. Enterprise reference velocity is notably faster than comparable US startups at this stage.

Series B AI / B2B

AvoMD

Clinical AI platform  ·  Series B  ·  KRW 15B

EHR-based AI that delivers real-time, evidence-based treatment protocol recommendations to physicians during consultations. Korea's structural advantage: hospital data is highly digitized and nationally interlinked, giving AvoMD a training data quality edge over global competitors. The timing aligns with rapidly maturing medical AI regulation in both Korea and the EU.
