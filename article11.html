The $100 Billion Problem With Consumer Research

Every major consumer goods company — from CJ Cheiljedang to L'Oréal to Unilever — runs hundreds of surveys every year. They recruit respondents, wait weeks for responses, spend millions on panels, and then extrapolate from a few hundred data points to decisions that affect supply chains, product launches, and marketing budgets worth billions. The whole process is slow, expensive, and structurally biased toward the kinds of consumers who respond to surveys.

The global market research industry is worth over $100 billion annually. And for decades, its core methodology — recruit humans, ask them questions, tabulate answers — has not fundamentally changed. Intellisia (인텔리시아) thinks AI can replace most of that stack.

Founded in February 2025 by CEO Baek Seung-guk (백승국) in Daejeon's KAIST district, Intellisia is Asia's first commercial deployments of AI synthetic consumers (합성소비자) — AI agents trained on real consumer behavior data that simulate how specific demographic segments respond to products, prices, and marketing stimuli. The company's platform, The Survey (더서베이), can generate responses from thousands of synthetic consumers in minutes.

"Traditional consumer research takes 4–6 weeks and costs millions. We do the same job in 30 minutes. The synthetic consumer isn't a replacement for all human insight — but it eliminates the 80% of research that's just waiting."

The Founder: A Serial Exit and a Second Bet

Baek Seung-guk is not a first-time founder. Before Intellisia, he built Dable (데이블) — a content recommendation and native advertising platform that used NLP to analyze media consumption across Asia, spanning 7 countries and thousands of publishers. In 2021, Yanolja acquired Dable, giving Baek a clean exit and, more importantly, deep expertise in training AI systems to model and predict human behavior at scale.

Intellisia is Baek's second act — and it is a direct extension of the same intellectual bet. Where Dable predicted which articles users would click, Intellisia predicts what consumers will buy, prefer, and say. The technical shift is from click-through prediction to full behavioral modeling. The market is larger, the use case is broader, and the moat — proprietary training data derived from real consumer surveys — compounds over time.

The founding team includes core engineers from Dable. The company was incorporated in February 2025 and by May 2025 had already launched the first version of The Survey — making it the first commercialized AI synthetic consumer platform in Asia.

The Technology: Synthetic Consumers, Not Chatbots

The core product is frequently misunderstood. Intellisia is not building a chatbot that answers survey questions. It is building consumer clones — AI agents that have been trained on real survey response datasets from specific demographic cohorts (age, gender, region, income, buying habits) and can then generate statistically consistent new responses as if those consumers were answering in real time.

The distinction matters. A chatbot improvises. A synthetic consumer has a learned behavioral profile: it doesn't know that it should say "I prefer organic" — it has internalized the conditional probability that consumers in its demographic cluster say "I prefer organic" given a set of prior purchase signals. The result is a system that reproduces survey distributions rather than individual guesses.

Intellisia's platform pipeline works as follows:

Data Layer
Consumer Behavioral Training
Real survey responses collected from partner panels are used to fine-tune large language models on consumer segment personas. Each persona encodes demographic attributes, purchase history, and attitudinal patterns into a latent behavioral profile. Training datasets span FMCG, beauty, food, and retail categories.
Simulation Layer
The Survey Platform
Clients upload survey instruments; the platform instantiates synthetic consumers matching the target demographic specification, runs the survey in parallel across thousands of agents, and returns response distributions with variance analysis. Turnaround: 30 minutes vs. 4–6 weeks for traditional panels. Cost: a fraction of human respondent recruitment.
Simulation Layer
Parastore — Retail Simulation
Launched as open-source in May 2026. Synthetic consumers navigate a 3D virtual store, pick up products, and make purchase decisions. The software enables retailers to simulate store layouts, shelf placements, and promotional scenarios before physical implementation. CJ Freshway validated the system for K-food retail planning in 2026.
Global Layer
ATLAS — 12-Country System
Launched May 2026. Covers Korea, US, UK, China, Germany, Japan, Australia, France, Canada, Brazil, and others. Each country's synthetic consumers are trained on locally collected survey data with country-specific demographic distributions and cultural consumption patterns. Accuracy vs. real surveys: 87–91% across 6 independent validation surveys.

Traction: 14 Conglomerates Before the Seed Round

The most unusual thing about Intellisia's fundraising story is the sequence. The company launched its product in May 2025 and, over the following six months, completed proof-of-concept validations with 14 major Korean conglomerates — including CJ Cheiljedang (CJ제일제당), Pulmuone (풀무원), and Fursys Group (퍼시스그룹) — before announcing its Seed round. That is an unusually long customer validation cycle for a pre-Seed company.

The sequencing was deliberate. CJ Cheiljedang's corporate accelerator program, Frontier Labs (프론티어랩스), selected Intellisia for its 5th cohort — one of the highest-signal endorsements a Korean B2B startup can receive. The program involves structured PoC projects with CJ business units, and Intellisia passed: it became the first startup in Frontier Labs history to complete an Asia-first technology validation (synthetic consumer for FMCG research).

The Seed round — led by CJ Investment (CJ인베스트먼트) and Sopoong Ventures (소풍벤처스) — closed in November 2025 at an undisclosed amount. The strategic logic of CJ Investment's participation is transparent: CJ Cheiljedang wants ongoing access to Intellisia's consumer simulation technology for product development and marketing.

Intellisia validated with 14 major conglomerates before closing its Seed. The list of PoC partners reads like a who's-who of Korean consumer goods — and they all paid to run experiments on synthetic consumers.

Government Validation: Deep Tech TIPS Fast Track

In September 2025, Intellisia was selected for the Ministry of SMEs and Startups Deep Tech TIPS Fast Track (딥테크 팁스 패스트트랙) program — one of Korea's most competitive government-backed R&D grant tracks, reserved for companies working on technology with verifiable deep-tech differentiation. The program provides up to ₩1.5 billion (~$1.1M USD) in R&D funding over three years, plus ₩200 million in commercialization support — ₩1.7 billion total.

TIPS selection matters as a signal for several reasons. The program has a low acceptance rate and requires peer review of the technology by an expert panel. Intellisia's selection in the LLM / synthetic consumer / dataset category means Korea's national innovation apparatus has independently assessed the technology as being genuinely novel — not just a ChatGPT wrapper.

The R&D funding from TIPS will specifically support Intellisia's LLM fine-tuning work on consumer behavioral datasets and proprietary dataset construction — the data moat that makes synthetic consumers accurate over time.

ATLAS: Going Global

The May 2026 launch of ATLAS represents Intellisia's first formal global expansion move. ATLAS stands for Automated Trans-market Language-unified Acceptance Study — a system that extends the synthetic consumer methodology across 12 countries, each trained on locally sourced consumer panels.

The accuracy validation is the most important part of the launch. Intellisia ran six independent surveys comparing synthetic consumer responses to real respondent data across the US, Japan, and China. The average reproduction rate was 88–90% — a level the company describes as sufficient for early-stage product and marketing research decisions where directional accuracy matters more than statistical precision.

The Competitive Landscape

Intellisia's closest global comparables are US-based startups like Aaru (San Francisco), which applies multi-agent simulation to B2B consumer research, and Chinese company Mirofish (미로피시). Both have raised capital and validated similar concepts — but neither has commercialized at the conglomerate-partnership level that Intellisia has achieved in Korea, nor do they hold the cultural and linguistic advantages Intellisia brings to Asian markets.

In Korea, the closest incumbent is OpenSurvey (오픈서베이) — a mobile-first survey platform with ~84 employees and a Series A raise. OpenSurvey is a human-panel platform, not a synthetic consumer system, which means it competes on speed and sampling efficiency rather than AI behavioral modeling. Intellisia's 30-minute turnaround is structurally faster than any human-panel approach, regardless of optimization.

The deeper competitive moat is data. Every survey Intellisia runs — whether a PoC or a production deployment — adds to the behavioral training corpus. The more consumer response data the company collects, the more accurate its synthetic consumers become. This creates a compounding data flywheel that makes the platform harder to replicate over time.

Why This Company Is Worth Watching
  • Serial founder credibility: Baek Seung-guk built and sold Dable to Yanolja. He knows how to build B2B data businesses in Asia — and the Intellisia thesis is a direct extension of that experience.
  • Enterprise validation before funding: 14 conglomerate PoCs before the Seed round is unusual. It means customer demand was demonstrated before capital was deployed — the opposite of the typical Korean startup fundraising sequence.
  • Deep Tech TIPS selection: Korea's most competitive government R&D program independently validated the technology. This is not a subsidy — it's a peer-reviewed assessment of novelty.
  • Data flywheel: Every survey run improves the training corpus. The accuracy of synthetic consumers compounds with usage — creating a durable moat against later-stage entrants.
  • ATLAS accuracy: 88–90% reproduction rates across three major global markets is commercially viable. It's not perfection, but it's sufficient for directional product and marketing research decisions.
  • Parastore signal: Open-sourcing Parastore is a developer ecosystem play — seeding adoption among retail and F&B companies before monetizing the enterprise tier. This is a textbook platform-scale strategy.

The Investment Thesis

Intellisia is a Seed-stage company with 12 employees, a single product line, and no disclosed revenue. The risks are real: large survey platform incumbents (Nielsen, Kantar) have AI research divisions; synthetic consumer accuracy at 89% may not satisfy clients requiring high statistical confidence; and the international ATLAS expansion requires localized sales infrastructure that a 12-person team does not yet have.

But the thesis is sound. Consumer research is one of the largest enterprise software budget lines that has not been disrupted by AI — precisely because it requires trust, not just speed. Intellisia spent its first six months building that trust, not raising capital. The CJ Investment and Sopoong Ventures backing provides strategic distribution into Korea's largest conglomerate ecosystem. The TIPS grant validates the technology at the national level.

For global VC and angel investors tracking Korea's AI sector, Intellisia represents something specific: a post-exit founder applying deep NLP expertise to a $100B market, with enterprise validation baked in before the Seed round closes, and a global expansion thesis grounded in quantified accuracy rather than just product ambition. The synthetic consumer category is emerging globally — and Intellisia is, as of mid-2026, Asia's most advanced commercial implementation of it.

Rebellions (리벨리온스)
Pre-IPO · AI Semiconductor
Korea's AI chip unicorn — $400M pre-IPO at a $2.34B valuation
Rebellions designs AI inference chips optimized for large language model workloads — the compute that runs inference on models like GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini at data center scale. The company's ATOM chip targets the gap between Nvidia's GPU dominance and the efficiency needs of inference-heavy deployments. In April 2026, Rebellions closed a $400M pre-IPO round at a $2.34B valuation, led by Mirae Asset Financial Group and the Korea National Growth Fund — with $166M coming directly from the Korean government. Total funding to date: ~$850M. IPO targeted for late 2026 or early 2027.
Why now: The National Growth Fund's direct government participation in a private tech company is unprecedented for Korea — it signals that Rebellions is considered strategic national infrastructure, not just a venture bet. With Nvidia supply constraints and rising inference costs, the AI chip alternative market is real and growing.
Wart Intelligence (워트인텔리전스)
Series B · IP / Patent AI
AI for the patent system — Korea's largest B2B IP software deal of 2026
Wart Intelligence builds AI-powered intellectual property management and analytics software for enterprises and law firms. The core product allows companies to search, analyze, and strategize around patent portfolios using LLM-based retrieval and classification — reducing the time attorneys and R&D teams spend on prior art searches, freedom-to-operate analyses, and patent landscape mapping. In July 2026, the company closed a ₩16.5B (~$12M) Series B — the largest single round in the Korean IP software sector on record. The round was led by domestic VCs with participation from IP-focused strategic investors.
Why now: IP litigation and patent strategy have become AI-critical workflows as companies race to protect AI-generated inventions and navigate the LLM training data copyright landscape. Wart Intelligence sits at the intersection of two fast-moving regulatory and commercial trends — patent volume explosion and AI-native legal tech adoption.
Seoul Dynamics (서울다이나믹스)
Series A · Physical AI
Physical AI for heavy industry — autonomous forklifts and cranes, built in Seoul
Seoul Dynamics develops Physical AI systems — AI-driven autonomous robots designed for heavy industrial environments: warehouses, logistics hubs, construction sites, and ports. Unlike consumer robotics, Seoul Dynamics focuses on high-payload, safety-critical scenarios where GPS-denied navigation and mixed-environment perception matter most. The company built a proprietary physical foundation model that enables robots to generalize across different warehouse layouts and load types without environment-specific retraining. In July 2026, the company raised ₩5.5B (~$4M) Series A led by G&Tech Venture Investment — validating the physical AI thesis in an industrial context where Korea's manufacturing and logistics density creates a natural proving ground.
Why now: Korea's labor shortage in logistics and manufacturing is acute — creating enterprise willingness to deploy autonomous heavy robots that would face regulatory friction in more cautious markets. Seoul Dynamics has a domestic proving ground that accelerates iteration speed and gives it a head start on safety certification relative to US or European competitors.
