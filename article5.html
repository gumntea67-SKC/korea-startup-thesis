DEEPX: The Apple M1 Architect Who Came Back to Build the ARM of Edge AI

Edge AI Chip NPU Pre-IPO Physical AI Series D

Lokwon Kim was one of eight engineers managing 3,000 developers when Apple designed the M1 chip. He brought the idea for his own edge AI company to Apple executives. They passed. So he returned to Korea, founded DEEPX in Pangyo in 2018, and spent six years building an NPU portfolio that delivers 25 TOPS at under 5 watts — a power-performance profile that makes Nvidia Jetson directly comparable. In April 2026 alone, DEEPX closed purchase orders equal to its entire 2025 revenue. Hyundai Robotics chose the DX-M1 as the "edge brain" for its next-gen robot platform. Series D is raising ₩600B at a ₩2.65 trillion pre-money valuation. The edge AI war is moving to hardware nobody talks about — and DEEPX is building the chip that runs it.

The Thesis: The Edge Is Where the AI War Is Won

The narrative around AI semiconductors has been dominated by data center hardware: Nvidia's H100, AMD's MI300, and a wave of Korean challengers — Rebellions, FuriosaAI — targeting inference workloads for large language models. That is a real and valuable market. It is also already crowded, capital-intensive, and shaped by foundry relationships that take years to build.

DEEPX is playing a different game. The company's core thesis is that the strategically decisive battle in AI semiconductors will be won at the edge — in the billions of devices that cannot connect to a cloud, cannot wait for round-trip latency, and cannot afford the power draw of a data center GPU. Cameras, robots, factories, autonomous vehicles, smart city infrastructure. These devices need AI that runs on-device, in real time, on a power budget measured in watts rather than kilowatts.

"The main battlegrounds in the AI era will move to the edge," CEO Lokwon Kim said in 2024. "Just as ARM dominated the CPU market with smartphones, the semiconductor company that dominates edge will dominate the AI market." The framing is deliberate. Kim is not trying to beat Nvidia. He is trying to build the ARM of the AI era — an architecture that wins by being everywhere Nvidia cannot go.

The Founder: The Man Who Helped Build Apple's M1

Lokwon Kim's professional biography reads like a roadmap for building a world-class semiconductor company. He worked at IBM TJ Watson Research Center, Cisco, and Broadcom before joining Apple — where he became one of eight core leaders managing a 3,000-person team during the development of the NPU inside the iPhone, and later co-designed the Apple M1, Apple's breakthrough in-house PC chip. He experienced the full cycle: architecture decisions, foundry negotiations, yield management, mass production ramp.

Kim brought his edge AI chip idea to Apple executives before leaving. They passed — Apple's business model depends on building chips exclusively for Apple devices, which is the opposite of what Kim wanted to build. So he returned to Korea in 2018 with an explicit goal: to be the "Morris Chang of Korea." Chang, the founder of TSMC, returned to Taiwan after working at Texas Instruments to build a foundry that gave the global semiconductor industry a new structural pillar. Kim wants DEEPX to play an analogous role in edge AI silicon — a company that makes the category accessible to device makers who cannot afford to design their own chips and cannot run Nvidia's Hopper in their products.

Why "ARM of Edge AI" is the right mental model

ARM didn't compete with Intel in CPUs. It built a low-power architecture for a different market — mobile — and won that market before Intel realized the threat. DEEPX is not trying to displace Nvidia in data centers. It is building an NPU optimized for the edge market: sub-5W power, $100-class price point, designed specifically for inference rather than training. Nvidia's Jetson line covers this market, but at higher power and cost. DEEPX's thesis is that there is 10-100x more volume at the edge than in data centers, and that a company that owns the dominant edge AI architecture will be more durable than one winning in the data center.

The Chip Portfolio: Four Chips, One Architecture

Most fabless NPU startups launch a single chip and iterate. DEEPX launched four at once — a deliberate architectural decision that reflects Kim's Apple experience. Different devices need fundamentally different compute profiles, and a single-chip company cannot serve CCTV cameras and industrial robots with the same silicon.

Chip Performance Power Target Application
DX-V1 Sub-5 TOPS <1W CCTV cameras, simple vision modules
DX-V3 ~10 TOPS <3W Smart cameras, video analytics
DX-M1 ★ 25 TOPS <5W Robots, smart factories, autonomous vehicles
DX-H1 22 POPS/server High Edge servers, workstations, multi-camera hubs

The DX-M1 is the commercial pivot. It entered mass production at the end of 2025, went to market validated by more than 300 companies across four years of customer development, and is positioned as a direct alternative to Nvidia's Jetson Orin Nano — with a 2–3× lower power envelope for comparable inference workloads. DEEPX claims design area one-third the size of competing NPUs and manufacturing cost similarly reduced by one-third, enabled by two proprietary technologies: IQ8 (an INT8 model compression technique that maintains accuracy while reducing compute) and Smart Memory Access (minimizes DRAM usage, critical for battery-powered and thermally constrained devices).

The DX-M2 is the next card: designed on Samsung's 2nm process, targeting mass production in early 2027. If Samsung's 2nm yield reaches Kim's publicly stated 70%+ target in early production, DEEPX will have a chip that leapfrogs the current generation of edge AI silicon on performance density.

The Anchor Customer: Hyundai Robotics Chooses DEEPX as Its "Edge Brain"

The most strategically significant event in DEEPX's recent history was not a funding announcement. It was a joint press release from CES Foundry 2026: Hyundai Motor Group's Robotics LAB, after three years of co-development (beginning with a 2023 MOU), has designated the DX-M1 as the "edge AI brain" for its next-generation on-device robot platform.

The technical context matters. Hyundai's robots — including DAL-e, its delivery robot, and Facey, its facial recognition technology — need to process vision AI tasks in real time without cloud connectivity. The DX-M1 enables the robot to recognize its surroundings, make autonomous navigation decisions, and respond to environmental changes without any network round trip, using less than 5 watts in operation. For a mobile robot, the power constraint is existential — it determines battery life and heat management across the entire platform.

The Hyundai partnership is more than a design win. It is a reference customer that de-risks the commercial story for every subsequent conversation DEEPX has with robot OEMs, automotive Tier 1s, and smart factory integrators. When Hyundai's robotics division — one of the most sophisticated in Korea — co-develops a chip platform over three years and commits to commercial production, it creates a validation signal that no amount of trade show demos can replicate.

The Physical AI timing advantage

Jensen Huang's June 2026 Korea visit — his second in seven months — brought Nvidia's physical AI narrative to a domestic audience that includes Hyundai, Kia, Samsung, LG, and the full stack of Korean industrial conglomerates. The DEEPX-Hyundai partnership was announced at CES Foundry 2026 in January, positioning DEEPX at the center of the physical AI conversation months before Jensen's visit. That positioning matters: Korean industrial buyers who are now searching for domestic edge AI silicon have a clear reference point.

Traction: Revenue Is Small, But the Velocity Signal Is Unmistakable

DEEPX's 2025 revenue was ₩33억 (₩3.3B, approximately $2.3M) — modest for a company with over ₩20B in annual operating losses. The financial picture at this stage is a standard chip startup profile: years of R&D spending before mass production, then a sharp revenue ramp once customer design-ins convert to purchase orders.

The signal that the ramp has begun: in April 2026 alone, DEEPX closed 18 purchase contracts from global customers — totaling a value roughly equal to all of 2025 revenue. These were not government demonstration projects or Korean conglomerate captive orders. They were commercial POs from global customers across smart factory, robotics, and AI sectors. The company's 2026 revenue target is ₩600억 (₩60B, ~$41M) — an 18× step-up from 2025 — driven primarily by DX-M1 volume orders ramping through H2 2026.

The Funding Picture: Korea's NPU Race Reaches Critical Mass

DEEPX is raising a Series D at a ₩2.65 trillion pre-money valuation ($1.8B), targeting ₩600B (~$415M) in new capital with an expected close in Q3 2026. Anchor investors are being assembled from Korea's major financial groups — KB Financial, Shinhan Financial, and NH Financial have each been reported as considering ₩100B commitments — alongside follow-on participation from existing investor AJU IB Investment. Skylake Equity Partners is also among the investors.

The ₩2.65T valuation represents a dramatic re-rating from the ₩700B valuation of the 2024 Series C — a 3.8× increase driven by the DX-M1 mass production milestone, the Hyundai design win, and the peer group premium established by Korean NPU competitors raising at even higher multiples. Rebellions (data center focus) and FuriosaAI (data center / LLM inference) have been valued at ₩3.4T and ₩3T respectively in recent rounds. The Korean AI chip category is being priced on optionality and first-mover advantage, not near-term revenue multiples.

An IPO follows the Series D — likely in 2026 or early 2027, on KOSDAQ or KOSPI. Korea's chipmaker IPO pipeline has created a competitive dynamic: Rebellions and FuriosaAI are also preparing for public listings, which creates urgency for DEEPX to establish its differentiation story (edge vs. data center) clearly before the public market has to make allocation decisions across all three.

Competitive Landscape: The Three-Way Korean NPU Race

Korea's AI chip ecosystem has produced three companies at material scale simultaneously — a coincidence of talent, government support, and Samsung foundry access that has no equivalent outside the US:

Company Focus Valuation Key Customer / Partner
Rebellions Data center NPU (LLM inference) ~₩3.4T KT, SK Telecom, strategic merger with Sapeon (Samsung)
FuriosaAI Data center NPU (LLM inference) ~₩3T Naver, LG CNS, Kakao
DEEPX Edge AI NPU (on-device inference) ₩2.65T (pre-D) Hyundai Robotics, POSCO DX, Jahwa Electronics

The positioning distinction matters enormously for investors. Rebellions and FuriosaAI are competing in the same market — large-scale LLM inference — against each other, against Nvidia's H100/H200, and against every hyperscaler's proprietary chip program. DEEPX is in a different market: on-device edge inference, where Nvidia Jetson is the primary incumbent but the overall market is orders of magnitude larger in unit volume.

Globally, the competitive landscape includes Hailo (Israeli, edge AI, similar positioning), Google Edge TPU, Intel Neural Network Processor, and Mythic (analog inference). None of these has won a major Korean industrial anchor customer. DEEPX's Hyundai partnership creates a reference that is hard for foreign competitors to replicate in the Korean market — and potentially gives DEEPX an introduction pathway into Hyundai's global supplier network.

Risks: The Gap Between Design Wins and Volume

Revenue ramp risk

The 18× revenue step-up from ₩3.3B to ₩60B in a single year is achievable only if the current PoC pipeline converts to production orders at high rates. Chip design wins typically take 6-18 months from validation to volume production ramp. If customer qualification cycles slip or if supply chain issues at Samsung affect DX-M1 availability, the 2026 target could slip significantly.

Samsung foundry dependency

Both the current DX-M1 and the next-gen DX-M2 are manufactured at Samsung. TSMC dominates the leading-edge foundry market at 3nm and below; Samsung's yield and capacity have historically been less predictable than TSMC's. The DX-M2's 2nm Samsung process is technically ambitious — Kim's stated 70% initial yield target is the minimum viable threshold for economical production. If Samsung's 2nm yields miss materially, DEEPX's timeline advantage over future competitors narrows.

Nvidia's competitive response

Nvidia's Jetson line is not standing still. The Jetson Orin family covers much of the edge AI performance range, and Nvidia's developer ecosystem — CUDA, TensorRT, Isaac for robotics — is vastly more mature than DEEPX's DXNN SDK. The primary DEEPX advantage is power efficiency and unit cost; if Nvidia narrows those gaps with Jetson successors, DEEPX's differentiation story weakens.

The critical question for global investors

Can DEEPX convert the Hyundai anchor into a global industrial customer base before the Series D capital runs out and before Nvidia's next Jetson generation reclaims the power efficiency gap? The answer depends on how fast the PoC pipeline converts to production POs, and on whether the DX-M2/Samsung 2nm bet produces a chip that is genuinely ahead of anything Nvidia can offer at the edge in 2027.

The Thesis: The Edge Has 100× the Volume — and One Clear Domestic Leader

Gartner estimates that by 2027, 70% of new IoT devices will embed AI capabilities. That implies billions of chips shipped annually into cameras, robots, vehicles, and industrial equipment — a market that dwarfs data center AI in unit volume, even if it is smaller in per-chip ASP. DEEPX is building for that market, with a portfolio validated by 300+ companies, an anchor partnership with one of Asia's most sophisticated robotics programs, and a CEO who has done this before at the largest consumer chip company in the world.

The company is pre-revenue at scale, burning ~₩20B per year, and dependent on a single foundry's 2nm execution. These are real risks. But the structural thesis — that edge AI inference is a large, underserved market where a power-efficient, low-cost NPU can build an ARM-style position — is intact, and the evidence from April 2026's order flow suggests the commercial turning point has arrived.

For global investors tracking Korea's AI hardware ecosystem: DEEPX is the company to watch if your thesis is that physical AI — robots, factories, autonomous systems — represents the next $1T wave in AI deployment. The edge AI chip is the component that makes physical AI real. DEEPX is building it. Hyundai is deploying it. The Series D is the last round before an IPO that will test whether Korea's industrial capital agrees.

Ones to watch  ·  Issue #005

Rebellions

Data center NPU (LLM inference)  ·  ₩3.4T valuation  ·  Pre-IPO  ·  Seoul

Rebellions is DEEPX's Korean NPU peer — and the most strategically interesting because of what happened in early 2026: it announced a merger with Sapeon, Samsung Electronics' in-house NPU unit. That transaction, if completed, would give Rebellions access to Samsung's foundry capacity on preferential terms, Samsung's enterprise sales channel, and a balance sheet that fundamentally changes the competitive equation. The company's ATOM chip targets data center inference — the Nvidia H100/H200 market — with a design led by engineers from SpaceX and leading US semiconductor firms. The ₩3.4T valuation reflects both the quality of the team and the optionality that a Samsung structural partnership provides. For global investors who want Korea AI chip exposure without the edge-specific thesis of DEEPX, Rebellions is the purest data center AI inference bet in the Korean market.

Pre-IPO AI Chip

Nota AI (노타)

AI model optimization / edge deployment  ·  Series B  ·  B2B SaaS  ·  Seoul

If DEEPX is the hardware, Nota AI is the software that makes AI models run efficiently on chips like the DX-M1. The company's NetsPresso platform automates the notoriously complex process of compressing and optimizing large AI models for deployment on edge devices — taking a model trained in PyTorch, quantizing it, pruning it, and converting it to run on specific NPU architectures with minimal accuracy loss. This problem is large and underappreciated: without model optimization, a standard computer vision model that runs well on a GPU may be 3–5× too large and too slow for an edge chip. Nota's software runs the optimization pipeline automatically, reducing a process that takes AI engineers weeks into a matter of hours. The company has integrations with multiple NPU vendors and is well-positioned to become the Arm-style "middleware" layer in the edge AI stack — owning the toolchain regardless of which hardware wins the chip war below it.

Series B AI Software

SAIGE Research (세이지리서치)

Industrial AI / predictive maintenance  ·  Series B  ·  B2B  ·  Seongnam

SAIGE Research builds AI-powered predictive maintenance and quality control systems for industrial manufacturers — the software that runs on edge AI chips like the DX-M1 inside smart factories. As Korea's manufacturing base (steel, semiconductors, automotive) invests heavily in AI-driven efficiency, SAIGE occupies the application layer of the physical AI stack: it takes real-time sensor and machine vision data from factory equipment, runs AI inference on-site using edge hardware, and alerts operators before equipment failures occur or before quality defects leave the line. The company counts POSCO (Korea's dominant steelmaker), Doosan, and several Samsung Display factories among its customers. Revenue has been compounding at 70%+ annually for three consecutive years — unusual durability for B2B industrial software. The risk is vertical focus: industrial AI deployments are slow to expand across geographies and manufacturing categories. But the same characteristic — deep integration with production-critical systems — creates switching costs that few software categories can match.
