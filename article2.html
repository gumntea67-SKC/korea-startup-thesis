Toss: The Dentist Who Dismantled Korea's Banking Oligopoly

Fintech First Profit 2024 Pre-IPO Founded 2013

Sending money in Korea used to take 30 clicks and three passwords. Lee Seung-gun — a dentist — spent eight months fighting regulators to change that with a three-tap wire transfer. A decade later, Toss is used by 60% of South Korea's population, crossed 2 trillion KRW in annual revenue in 2024, posted its first full-year profit, and is targeting a $10B+ US IPO in 2026. The thesis isn't about a single product. It's about what happens when you rebuild the entire financial stack from scratch in a market that never had a real alternative.

The Problem: A Market Built for Friction

To understand why Toss exists, you need to understand what Korean banking looked like before it. Korea has five dominant commercial banks — KB Kookmin, Shinhan, Hana, Woori, and NH Agricultural — that collectively control the vast majority of deposits and lending. They are profitable, entrenched, and not particularly motivated to improve user experience. Sending money between accounts required navigating a desktop application, downloading a security plugin, entering multiple passwords, and confirming via a secondary authentication device.

The problem wasn't unique to Korea, but Korea had a specific aggravating factor: regulatory capture. The incumbents had used their influence to construct a compliance environment that made new entrants nearly impossible to license. When Lee Seung-gun tried to obtain a small payments license in 2013, he was rejected eight times over eight months. Regulators kept citing rules that didn't exist in writing or applying standards inconsistently. He only got through by physically camping at the Financial Services Commission with lawyers.

The structural moat Toss is attacking

Korea's five largest banks collectively hold over 60% of retail deposits and were established between 1897 and 1967. They have never faced a serious domestic challenger. Their combined NIM advantage over Toss Bank currently stands at approximately 80–120 basis points — not because their cost of capital is lower, but because their customers have nowhere else to go.

The Founder: Eight Rejections Before the First Approval

Lee Seung-gun graduated from Seoul National University School of Dentistry — Korea's most prestigious — and practiced as a dentist for a brief period before deciding his real interest was software. He had no banking background. He had no connections at the Financial Services Commission. What he had was a specific, visceral frustration with how difficult it was to send someone money.

His first product — a simple peer-to-peer wire transfer app requiring nothing more than a phone number and a single PIN — launched in 2015 after that eight-month approval fight. It spread almost entirely by word of mouth. Within three months, Toss had processed over 100 billion KRW in transfers. The banks noticed. Several attempted to build competing products; none launched in time to matter.

Lee's operating thesis from the beginning was not "build a payments company." It was "become the financial OS of Korean consumers." Every product since — credit scores, stock trading, insurance, banking — has been designed around a single user experience layer with Toss at the center.

The Business: Five Companies Inside One App

Toss is not a payments company. It is a holding company — Viva Republica — operating five distinct regulated financial businesses that share a single user interface. This is the structural reality that most Western observers miss when they describe it as a "super app." Each subsidiary is independently capitalized, independently licensed, and independently profitable (or on track to be):

The integration matters more than it looks. A user who opens a Toss Bank savings account is immediately shown their Toss Securities portfolio alongside it. A user who checks their credit score on the Toss app is automatically offered a pre-approved loan from Toss Bank at a better rate than the incumbent banks can match — because Toss has better behavioral data. The product is cross-selling at a scale that conventional financial institutions cannot replicate without cannibalizing their own fee structures.

The engagement flywheel

Toss's 25M monthly active users open the app an average of 10+ times per day. For context, major Korean banks report average app sessions of 1–2 per week. The engagement gap isn't explained by features alone — it's explained by Toss's position as the financial aggregation layer. Users check their consolidated net worth, spending, credit score, and investment performance in one place. Each check is a data point. Each data point improves the next offer. The flywheel compounds.

The Numbers: What the P&L Actually Says

The headline story — first full-year profit in 2024 — understates what happened. The more important number is the trajectory. From a 216.6B KRW net loss in 2023 to a 21.3B KRW net profit in 2024 represents a ~238B KRW swing in twelve months. In 2025, net profit grew approximately 847% year-over-year to ~220B KRW.

The operating leverage is becoming visible. Revenue grew 38% in 2025 while operating costs grew at a materially slower pace. Toss Bank's NIM — net interest margin — ran at approximately 3.2 percentage points for household loans, well above what the large commercial banks can offer borrowers. The credit default rate has stayed within acceptable bounds even as the loan book grew, which validates the proprietary credit scoring model built on behavioral data rather than traditional bureau scores.

The Q2 2025 standalone quarter showed ₩668B in consolidated revenue — a 41% year-over-year increase — suggesting the growth rate has not materially decelerated. At this run rate, the company exits 2025 at an annualized revenue pace approaching $2B USD.

The IPO: Why the US, and Why Now

Viva Republica dropped its Korean IPO plan in late 2024 and pivoted to a US listing. The rationale is straightforward: Korea's equity markets do not know how to price a financial services technology company. The KOSPI and KOSDAQ apply traditional bank P/B multiples to internet-native financial businesses, which consistently undervalues the embedded growth and network effects. Nubank on NYSE trades at a premium to book value that no Korean internet bank would receive on KOSDAQ.

The US IPO — targeting a raise of $2–3B at a $10B+ valuation — would make it the largest US debut by a Korean company since Coupang raised $4.6B in March 2021. SEC talks began in early 2026. A confidential S-1 filing was expected in Q1 2026 with a public offering targeting mid-year.

Valuation sanity check

At a $10B valuation against ~$1.8B in 2025 revenue, Toss would trade at roughly 5.5x EV/Revenue. Nubank currently trades at ~6x forward revenue. Block (formerly Square) trades at ~2x. Chime, the closest US private comp, was last valued at ~$25B at lower absolute revenue. The $10B number implies Toss deserves a premium to Block and a discount to Nubank — arguably defensible given Korea's smaller TAM but higher market penetration.

The Bear Case: What Could Actually Go Wrong

Three real risks deserve attention, none of which are commonly discussed.

Regulatory reversal. Toss's growth has depended in part on a regulatory environment that loosened over a decade. Korea's FSC has shown willingness to re-tighten household lending caps, which directly affects Toss Bank's NIM and loan growth. In Q1 2026, Toss Bank's corporate loan balance declined 7.5% under new household debt guidelines. The company has responded by diversifying into SME lending, but the regulatory ceiling is real and non-trivial.

KakaoBank competition. KakaoBank — already publicly listed on KOSPI — hit record profits in 2025 and has 23M+ customers. It shares many of Toss's advantages (digital-native UX, no branch costs) while benefiting from the Kakao ecosystem's 47M monthly active users across Kakao Talk. Toss has higher engagement per user; KakaoBank has a larger distribution moat through the messaging layer.

International expansion cost. Toss has begun exploring Southeast Asia (notably Vietnam), which is structurally similar to early-2010s Korea in terms of underbanked population and bad incumbent UX. But replicating the regulatory fight in each new market is expensive and slow. If international expansion absorbs significant capital post-IPO without near-term revenue contribution, it will test US investor patience.

The Bull Case: The Inflection Has Already Happened

The bears focus on risks that are, to a meaningful degree, already priced. The overlooked upside is operational leverage at scale.

Toss spent 2016–2023 building infrastructure: obtaining licenses, hiring risk teams, building the credit bureau, absorbing early-year losses at Toss Bank. That investment phase is substantially complete. The five subsidiaries are now either profitable or on a defined path to profitability. What remains is harvesting the distribution: 30 million registered users who already trust Toss with their financial data, across whom Toss has only begun to cross-sell.

The Toss Securities attachment rate among Toss Bank customers, for instance, remains low — which is an opportunity, not a ceiling. The auto-insurance distribution through Toss Insurance has barely launched. The credit bureau subsidiary is early-innings. Each of these is a revenue line that doesn't require new customer acquisition — it requires deepening engagement with users who are already in the app ten times a day.

What to watch before the IPO

1. Q3/Q4 2025 NIM trend — Does Toss Bank sustain 3.2%+ NIM as household lending caps tighten? Compression here is the single most important leading indicator for bank-segment valuation.

2. S-1 disclosure — The confidential filing will reveal cohort retention data and cross-sell attachment rates for the first time. These numbers will determine whether $10B is the floor or the ceiling.

3. Southeast Asia capex guidance — How much post-IPO capital is earmarked for international expansion, and on what timeline? The answer determines whether Toss is a Korea profitability story or a global growth story — two very different valuation frameworks.

The Bottom Line

Toss is not a bet on a product. It is a bet on a company that has built the financial infrastructure layer for a 52-million-person economy — and done so with better unit economics than the incumbents who had a century head start. The 2024 profit inflection wasn't a milestone; it was confirmation that the cost structure is sustainable. The 2025 acceleration was confirmation that the model compounds.

Whether the US IPO prices at $10B or $15B is a secondary question. The primary question — whether Toss will be the dominant financial services platform in Korea for the next two decades — has already been answered by the behavior of its 30 million users, who open the app more than any bank in the country has ever achieved.

The dentist got eight rejections and kept going. That stubbornness is now a $10 billion company.
