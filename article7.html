The Problem No One Could Measure

When a patient comes in with high blood pressure, you put a cuff on their arm and get a number. With diabetes, you prick a finger and read a glucose level. The numbers guide the prescription, monitor the treatment, and tell you whether the drug is working.

Now imagine you're a psychiatrist. Your patient says they feel depressed. You administer the PHQ-9 questionnaire — a 9-item self-report. You observe their behavior. You ask about sleep, appetite, energy. Everything you know about this patient's mental state comes through language and self-perception. There is no equivalent of the blood pressure cuff. There is no number.

This was the frustration that drove Jeon Hong-jin for 27 years. As a Professor of Psychiatry at Samsung Seoul Hospital and Associate Dean for Research at Sungkyunkwan University Medical School, he built one of Korea's most-cited research programs on depression and anxiety — and never stopped asking why mental health, unlike every other branch of medicine, remained stubbornly unmeasurable.

In June 2022, after three decades in academic medicine, he founded Meditrics (메디트릭스). The product he set out to build was a device that would do for depression what the glucometer did for diabetes: give patients and clinicians an objective number, in real time, and turn that number into a therapeutic intervention.

"Blood pressure and blood sugar can be measured by a machine — but mental health is a subjective domain. Meditrics exists to change that."

The Founder's Credential Stack

Jeon Hong-jin is not a typical tech founder. He is one of Korea's most prominent psychiatric researchers — the kind of clinician-scientist who publishes in JAMA Psychiatry while also running a full patient load. He holds both an MD and PhD, and his institutional affiliations are a signal of standing in Korean academic medicine that global investors should not underestimate.

Samsung Seoul Hospital (삼성서울병원) consistently ranks as Korea's top or second-ranked hospital. The Sungkyunkwan University Medical School that houses it is the country's most research-productive. Jeon serves simultaneously as a clinical professor, Associate Dean for Research at the medical school, and Director of the hospital's Digital Therapy Research Center — a triple role that gives him direct access to patients, research infrastructure, and institutional purchasing channels.

The founding of Meditrics was enabled by the Ministry of Health and Welfare's "K-DEM Station" research-centered hospital commercialization program, which brought together 27 companies and researchers to develop digital therapeutics and electronic medicine. Meditrics was one of the startups that emerged from this program — a signal of government-backed clinical legitimacy that is unusual for a seed-stage company.

MindChair: How It Works

The flagship product is the MindChair (마인드체어), classified by Korea's MFDS as a medical biofeedback device (의료용바이오피드백장치). It is not an app or a wearable. It is a physical chair.

The patient sits in a motion chair equipped with biometric sensors that measure real-time physiological signals — electroencephalography (EEG), galvanic skin response, heart rate variability, and respiration. The patient puts on VR goggles and enters an immersive environment designed to guide relaxation and biofeedback training. As the patient's physiological state changes, the motion chair responds — tilting, vibrating, and adjusting to reinforce the feedback loop. The VR environment visualizes the patient's own autonomic nervous system in real time, teaching them to regulate the signals they can now see.

The result is a device that does four things simultaneously: self-assessment (baseline measurement of depression and anxiety levels), treatment (biofeedback relaxation protocol), monitoring (session-by-session tracking), and sleep intervention (autonomic regulation for insomnia). The hardware was co-developed with CJ 4DPlex (Korea's 4D cinema motion platform company), Medicore, and BrainU.

The clinical case for MindChair was established in a 118-patient trial. Participants with clinically significant depression and anxiety underwent four weeks of MindChair sessions. The results showed statistically significant improvement across depression scores, anxiety scores, and sleep quality measures. This is the clinical foundation for the regulatory approval process now underway with Korea's MFDS (Ministry of Food and Drug Safety / 식약처).

The Business Model: From ICU to Living Room

Meditrics is building what it calls a three-tier distribution model, designed to follow the patient journey across the Korean healthcare system:

Tier 1 (B2B, tertiary hospitals / 3차 병원): The initial placement target is tertiary hospitals — specifically psychiatry departments, oncology centers (where cancer patients frequently experience comorbid depression and anxiety), and comprehensive health checkup centers. These are high-dwell-time, medically supervised environments where a prescription-grade VR biofeedback chair can be deployed, billable, and monitored.

Tier 2 (B2B, general hospitals / 2차 종합병원): The expansion layer is general hospitals with inpatient psychiatry and neurology wards. Higher volume, somewhat lower acuity.

Tier 3 (B2B1C, primary care clinics / 1차 의료기관): The long-term opportunity is primary care — family medicine clinics, pediatric offices, rehabilitation centers, and private health checkup centers. This layer converts MindChair from a hospital device into a widely distributed wellness infrastructure point.

Running in parallel to the hardware rollout, Meditrics is developing a mobile digital therapeutic — a smartphone and smartwatch version of the biofeedback protocol that enables home-based treatment after an initial hospital session. A consumer wellness version (OTC, no prescription required) is also in development for the post-MFDS-approval phase.

The logic is borrowed from how continuous glucose monitors moved from hospital wards to wrists: establish clinical credibility at the hospital level, then push the technology toward the patient's daily life.

The Regulatory Tailwind: Korea's DTx Law

In December 2023, Korea's National Assembly passed the Digital Medical Products Act (DMPA), which took effect in January 2025. Korea became the first country in the world to enact a standalone law specifically governing digital therapeutic devices — creating a defined regulatory approval pathway, a reimbursement category, and a national certification framework for DTx companies that had not previously existed.

Before the DMPA, Korean DTx companies operated in regulatory ambiguity: a software-based therapy could be classified as a medical device, a wellness product, or nothing at all, depending on which desk at the MFDS happened to review the application. The DMPA eliminated that ambiguity. It also created the political infrastructure for insurance reimbursement — the make-or-break element for any B2B healthcare device in Korea's single-payer system.

For Meditrics, the timing is close to optimal. The company completed its clinical trial and obtained NEP certification before the DMPA took effect. It is now pursuing formal MFDS device approval under the new framework, positioning it to be among the first hardware DTx companies to receive approval and billing codes under the new law.

Why This Matters for Global Investors

Investment Signal Checklist
  • Korea's digital mental health market is growing at 18.5% CAGR — from $555M (2024) to $3.6B by 2035. Global mental health apps market hits $40.9B by 2035.
  • Korea has some of the highest depression and suicide rates in the OECD; 25% of adults have experienced a depressive or anxiety disorder, but only 12.1% ever sought treatment — structural demand for accessible intervention.
  • Reimbursement pathway now exists via the January 2025 Digital Medical Products Act — the critical missing piece for B2B DTx monetization in Korea's public healthcare system.
  • Hardware moat: most mental health tech companies are apps. Meditrics is a certified medical device requiring clinical deployment — harder to copy, stickier once placed, and positioned to command insurance billing codes.
  • Founder-clinician advantage: Jeon's access to Samsung Seoul Hospital's patient population, research protocols, and institutional procurement channels provides a clinical validation loop that a non-medical founder could not replicate.
  • Samsung C-Lab pedigree accelerates US/Japan/EU introductions via Samsung's existing MedTech distribution relationships.

Risks to Watch

MFDS device approval is still pending as of this writing. The timeline for prescription-grade hardware approval in Korea is historically 18–36 months from clinical trial completion, and any delays affect the reimbursement clock. Hardware B2B sales cycles are measured in quarters, not weeks — hospital procurement committees are slow and capital-constrained. Revenue at ₩498M (2024) is pre-commercial; the Series A runway buys time for approval, not sustained growth.

Competitive risk comes from pure-software DTx platforms (much faster to scale, lower marginal cost) and from global incumbents that may enter the Korean market following the DMPA's passage. Meditrics's defense is clinical credibility — the 27-year research pedigree, the Samsung Seoul Hospital anchoring, and the hardware's biometric data moat that pure-software competitors cannot easily replicate.

Three companies in Korea's mental health tech stack worth watching

Meditrics sits at the hardware-clinical end of a broader digital mental health ecosystem that is maturing rapidly in Korea. Here are three companies that complement, compete with, or are converging toward similar territory.

WELT Corp Series C
Korea's insomnia DTx — approved, reimbursed, and scaling
WELT Corp is the company closest to proving that prescription digital therapeutics can build a sustainable business in Korea. Founded by physician Sean Kang, who trained at Harvard's School of Public Health, WELT-I became Korea's second approved digital therapeutic in April 2023 — a cognitive behavioral therapy app for insomnia prescribed and billed like a drug. The company subsequently raised €19 million in Series C funding and has been expanding into Japan and Southeast Asia. The significance: WELT-I demonstrated that the MFDS approval + insurance reimbursement loop actually works for software-based DTx. Meditrics is attempting to prove the same loop for hardware.
Signal: €19M Series C. Korea's insomnia market is a natural on-ramp for Meditrics's sleep module — watch for potential clinical collaboration.
Atommerce (아토머스) Series C planned H2 2026
Korea's largest mental health platform — going employer-first
Atommerce built the largest virtual therapy and digital mental health benefit platform in Korea, operating at the intersection of B2C counseling and B2B employer wellness. With $23.2M raised through Series B and a Series C on the calendar for H2 2026, it is the closest thing Korea has to a full-stack digital mental health company. Where Meditrics is building diagnostic and therapeutic hardware for clinical settings, Atommerce is building access infrastructure for the population that will never walk into a psychiatrist's office — the addressable market below the clinical threshold, which is vastly larger.
Signal: Series C round expected H2 2026. Employer mental health benefits is one of the fastest-growing segments in Korean HR tech as chaebols respond to government wellness mandates.
MindCafe (마인드카페) Scaled B2C
Korea's largest digital mental health community — 2M+ users
MindCafe is Korea's largest digital mental health counseling platform, connecting users with licensed therapists and peer support communities at consumer price points. With over 2 million registered users, it occupies the top-of-funnel in Korea's mental health access stack — the place where Koreans first acknowledge they might need support, before they walk into a clinical setting. The platform is the behavioral evidence layer that systemic players like Meditrics and Atommerce are ultimately trying to serve with hardware and employer benefits: the 24.4 million Korean adults who have experienced depression or anxiety but have never sought formal treatment.
Signal: The gap between the 25% of Korean adults with a mental health diagnosis and the 12.1% who seek treatment represents roughly 12 million reachable users. MindCafe's B2C data is the demand signal that infrastructure investors should be watching.
