S2W: The KAIST Lab That Mapped the Dark Web for INTERPOL

Cybersecurity AI KOSDAQ IPO 2025 B2G / Defense Founded 2018

Most companies claim to use AI. S2W built DarkBERT — a language model trained entirely on dark web data — published it at ACL 2023, and handed it to INTERPOL. The Seongnam-based startup spent its first seven years as the only Korean cybersecurity firm that foreign governments and intelligence agencies trusted enough to sign contracts with. It listed on KOSDAQ in September 2025 with a 1,972-to-1 subscription ratio, pulling in ₩5.14 trillion in bids for a sub-₩100B deal. Now it is pursuing what it calls the "Palantir of Asia" playbook — Japan, Singapore, Southeast Asia — and betting that the dark web intelligence moat it has spent seven years building is worth far more outside Korea than inside it.

The Problem: Intelligence That Lives Where No One Looks

Most of the world's most valuable threat intelligence isn't on Google. It lives on Tor-routed dark web forums where ransomware gangs auction stolen credentials, on Telegram channels where nation-state operators coordinate, and in cryptocurrency transaction graphs where money-laundering flows are traceable if you know how to read them. Enterprises and governments are perpetually a step behind because the data is structurally inaccessible — it requires constant scraping across thousands of constantly-changing onion addresses, translation of jargon that shifts weekly, and graph analysis connecting pseudonymous identities across platforms.

Existing solutions in 2018 were largely built by Western cybersecurity firms — Recorded Future, Flashpoint, Digital Shadows — that had designed their products for enterprise IT security teams, not for law enforcement or national intelligence operations. They were expensive, English-centric, and not optimized for the specific threats most relevant to Asian governments: North Korean crypto theft operations, Chinese APT groups, and Asian-language cybercrime forums that Western tools barely indexed. The gap wasn't a product gap. It was a data and research gap.

Why the dark web is structurally different from the surface web

Dark web forums use constantly evolving slang, encrypted messaging norms, and are deliberately designed to evade automated crawling. A standard BERT model trained on Wikipedia and news text achieves under 60% accuracy classifying illegal content on dark web forums. DarkBERT, trained on 5.83GB of actual dark web text, achieves 90%. That gap is not a model architecture improvement — it's a data moat.

The Founders: When a Security Professor Needs a CEO

S2W's origin story is unusually clean for a deep-tech startup: a researcher who had the insight but not the business experience found someone who had the business experience but was looking for a deep-tech problem worth his time.

Shin Seung-won is a professor at KAIST's Graduate School of Information Security, widely considered Korea's top academic authority on dark web analysis. His lab had been crawling and indexing dark web data for years, producing research that Korean law enforcement agencies increasingly relied on but couldn't operationalize at scale. The data existed; the productization didn't. In 2018, Shin approached Seo Sang-deok — a KAIST electrical engineering graduate who had subsequently gotten an MBA from Michigan and had prior startup experience — with a direct proposition: co-found a company around the lab's dark web research.

Seo's background is unusual for a deep-tech CEO. He is not a researcher. He is explicitly an operator: someone who can translate academic IP into government contracts, manage investor relations, and build the kind of institutional credibility that gets a Korean startup into INTERPOL's partner program. S2W was incorporated in September 2018 in Seongnam, near KAIST's Seoul campus, with the lab's dark web corpus and research methodology as its primary asset.

Founder profiles

Seo Sang-deok (CEO): KAIST EE → Michigan MBA. Operator background with prior startup experience. Selected as a WEF Technology Pioneer in 2023. Drives B2G sales and global expansion strategy.

Shin Seung-won (Co-founder / CSO): KAIST professor, Korea's leading dark web researcher. Provides the academic credibility and research pipeline that underpins S2W's technical moat. ACL 2023 co-author on DarkBERT paper.

The n번방 Moment: From Lab Project to National Priority

S2W's profile inside Korea changed permanently in 2020 with the investigation of what became known as the n번방 (Room N) case — one of the most widely covered criminal investigations in Korean history. A Telegram-based network had been operating across dozens of encrypted chat rooms, systematically blackmailing and sexually exploiting women and minors. The operators were sophisticated enough to use cryptocurrency for payments and to cycle through new channels rapidly to evade detection.

Korean law enforcement was struggling to map the network's structure. S2W's XARVIS platform — which had been quietly indexing Telegram groups, tracing cryptocurrency flows, and building identity graphs across anonymous channels — was brought in. The platform's ability to connect pseudonymous Telegram identities to real-world blockchain transactions, and to cross-reference activity patterns across channels that had already been shut down, provided the investigative leads that eventually led to arrests. The investigation resulted in a 40-year sentence for the principal operator — the longest sentence for a digital sex crime in Korean history at the time.

The n번방 case did something that no product demo could: it demonstrated at national scale that S2W's data infrastructure was operational, not theoretical. It also established a template the company has since replicated — selling not a software license but an investigative capability to institutions that need intelligence outcomes, not dashboards.

Why the n번방 case mattered for the business

Government contracts in the security sector are driven by demonstrated outcomes, not specifications. A single high-profile case reference — one that is publicly known, judicially confirmed, and politically significant — is worth years of pilot programs. S2W has used the n번방 reference in every subsequent B2G sales motion. It is the reason the company won contracts in Japan, Singapore, and Taiwan before most Western cybersecurity firms had heard of it.

The Technology: DarkBERT and the AI Moat

S2W's most technically significant asset is DarkBERT, the world's first large language model trained specifically on dark web data. The model was developed jointly with KAIST and published at ACL 2023 — the most competitive venue in natural language processing — under the title "DarkBERT: A Language Model for the Dark Side of the Internet."

The technical approach is straightforward in concept but expensive in execution: take RoBERTa (Facebook's optimized BERT variant), and continue pre-training it on 5.83GB of filtered dark web text rather than Wikipedia and news corpora. Training ran for approximately 15 days on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. The result is a model that understands the lexical conventions, coded language, and structural patterns of dark web communication at a level that general-purpose LLMs cannot match.

Peer-reviewed, reproducible

The academic publication is not incidental to the business. In B2G sales — particularly to defense ministries, intelligence agencies, and international organizations — peer-reviewed, reproducible research is a procurement prerequisite in a way that it never is in commercial software sales. DarkBERT's ACL paper functions as a technical certification. It is the reason S2W can sit across from a procurement officer at Singapore's Ministry of Defense and explain its methodology in terms that a technical review committee can validate independently.

The model is now the intelligence backbone of S2W's core products. DarkBERT powers DarkCHAT (an AI chatbot interface for querying dark web intelligence), DarkINTEL (an incident monitoring and filtering layer), and the cross-domain analysis engine that connects activity across dark web forums, Telegram, and cryptocurrency transaction graphs.

The Product Stack: Two Platforms, Two Markets

S2W has rationalized its product line around two distinct platforms serving two distinct buyer profiles. Understanding the separation is important for evaluating the company's unit economics and expansion thesis.

QUAXAR Enterprise security teams, CISOs Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) platform DRP + ASM + TI unified, external attack surface monitoring

XARVIS is S2W's flagship and its primary moat. It collects and indexes dark web forums, dark web marketplaces, Telegram, and other hidden channels in real time. Its DarkBERT-powered analysis layer classifies content, extracts entities, and builds relationship graphs that connect pseudonymous identities across platforms. The INTERPOL contract is built on XARVIS. So are contracts with Japanese and Taiwanese government agencies.

QUAXAR is the enterprise play — a Cyber Threat Intelligence platform that integrates digital risk protection, attack surface management, and threat intelligence into a single interface. It is a more competitive market (Recorded Future, CrowdStrike Falcon, Mandiant Advantage are all alternatives), but it serves as a revenue diversifier and enterprise pipeline that can be sold alongside, or as an entry point toward, XARVIS for customers whose threat profiles escalate over time.

The B2G advantage: why government contracts compound differently than enterprise SaaS

Enterprise SaaS churns. Government contracts renew. A B2G relationship at a law enforcement agency or defense ministry that starts with a pilot typically deepens over years as the agency's reliance on the platform increases and switching costs rise. S2W's Japan contract — initially a limited engagement — was renewed in 2026 at significantly higher value. Government buyers also provide something commercial customers rarely do: referenceable credibility that unlocks other government buyers in adjacent jurisdictions.

The Customers: From INTERPOL to Singapore MoD

S2W's customer roster is its most defensible asset and the clearest signal of product-market fit in a market where most competitors are pitching aspirationally. The company has signed contribution agreements or active contracts with:

INTERPOL

First and only Korean cybersecurity company in the Gateway Initiative — INTERPOL's public-private intelligence cooperation program. S2W provides dark web data and DarkBERT-powered analysis to support multinational cybercrime investigations. The contribution agreement was formalized in January 2022; S2W was invited to speak at the 2025 INTERPOL conference. The relationship gives S2W access to intelligence from 195 member countries, creating a feedback loop that improves its own data coverage.

Japan

Multi-agency engagement, contract renewed and expanded in 2026. S2W signed its first Japan government agency contract before its KOSDAQ listing and has since expanded. A local subsidiary is being established in H1 2026 to serve as the regional base. Japan is S2W's most advanced international market — the country has been rapidly increasing its cybersecurity budget under the Kishida and subsequent administrations, and its government agencies have been more willing than most Asian counterparts to rely on foreign-developed AI tools when those tools have strong academic validation.

Singapore

Singapore Ministry of Defense — one of the most credentialed defense buyers in Southeast Asia. The Singapore MoD contract validates S2W's positioning as a legitimate defense-sector vendor, not just a commercial cybersecurity tool. Singapore serves as the planned hub for S2W's Southeast Asia expansion.

Taiwan

Taiwan Stock Exchange. Financial sector cyber intelligence — monitoring for market manipulation, insider trading leak signals, and financial crime on dark web channels. The Taiwan contract demonstrates S2W's ability to sell into regulated financial institutions, expanding the addressable market beyond pure law enforcement.

Korea

Korean National Police Agency, defense agencies, multiple financial institutions. The domestic base is well-established but not the growth story — Korea is a reference market, not the expansion thesis.

The IPO: A 1,972-to-1 Bet on the Dark Web

S2W listed on KOSDAQ on September 19, 2025, under ticker 488280, via the technology-growth company special listing track (기술성장기업 특례상장) — a pathway designed for pre-profit companies with validated technology. The IPO was not a quiet event.

The offering price was set at ₩13,200 — the upper bound of the desired range. In the general subscription conducted September 10–11, S2W recorded a competition ratio of 1,972 to 1, with subscription deposits totaling ₩5.14 trillion (approximately $3.7 billion) against an offering that raised roughly $70–80M. The ratio placed it among the most sought-after Korean tech IPOs of 2025.

Why did a relatively small cybersecurity company with operating losses attract that level of retail and institutional interest? Three reasons are apparent in hindsight. First, the B2G customer roster was unusually credible for a company of S2W's size — INTERPOL and Singapore MoD are not names that appear in most Korean startup cap tables. Second, the "Palantir of Asia" positioning resonated in a market that had watched Palantir's US market cap appreciate from its 2020 DPO price to over $100B. Third, and most practically: there was no comparable listed pure-play in Korean dark web AI. S2W had no domestic comp.

The Palantir of Asia comparison — how fair is it?

Palantir built its business on US government contracts (CIA, NSA, DoD) that provided both revenue and the institutional legitimacy to expand into commercial markets. S2W is running the same playbook in Asia: start with B2G intelligence contracts in jurisdictions with serious cybersecurity budgets (Japan, Singapore, Korea), use those references to open commercial doors, and build a data moat through the government work that makes the commercial product better. The analogy holds structurally. The execution risk is whether Asian B2G contract cycles and budget sizes can sustain the same growth trajectory that Palantir achieved with US defense spending.

The Bear Case

Any honest analysis of S2W requires stating the risks clearly. There are three that matter.

Revenue concentration

S2W's revenue is heavily concentrated in a small number of large government contracts. The Japan renewal in 2026 is encouraging, but if that contract or the Singapore MoD engagement were to not renew or be rebid competitively, the growth thesis would require significant revision. The 93% 2026 revenue growth forecast is only plausible if current pipeline contracts close on schedule.

Competition from well-capitalized Western firms

Recorded Future (acquired by Mastercard for $2.65B in 2024), CrowdStrike, and Mandiant all have dark web monitoring products and far larger sales and marketing budgets than S2W. As Asian government cybersecurity budgets grow, these firms will compete more aggressively in the same jurisdictions S2W is targeting. S2W's edge — DarkBERT, academic credibility, the n번방 reference, INTERPOL partnership — is real but not permanent. The window for establishing market position before well-resourced Western competitors take it seriously is finite.

KOSDAQ stock volatility

S2W's KOSDAQ market cap has ranged between roughly ₩140B and ₩300B since listing — a 2x band of uncertainty that reflects how thin the liquidity is and how sensitive the stock is to any piece of contract news. For global investors evaluating the company, the public market valuation is a reference point rather than a reliable anchor. The IPO subscription frenzy created an opening-day premium that has since partially unwound. This is normal for Korean tech listings but creates a challenging entry point for institutional capital.

The key question for international investors

Can S2W replicate Palantir's B2G compounding in Asia before a better-capitalized Western competitor decides Asia is worth prioritizing? The answer depends almost entirely on contract execution in Japan and Singapore over the next 18 months. If those two markets produce the reference customers S2W needs, the moat widens. If not, the company faces a much harder competitive environment with a balance sheet that, post-IPO, is substantially less flush than Palantir's was when it began its growth phase.

The Thesis: A Data Moat That Governments Can't Build Themselves

Here is why S2W is interesting to watch despite the risks: governments cannot build what S2W has built.

Dark web intelligence infrastructure requires years of continuous crawling, model training, identity resolution, and operational refinement. A defense ministry can hire security researchers, but it cannot accumulate seven years of dark web corpus data and the INTERPOL-validated investigative track record that comes with it. The product S2W sells is not primarily software — it is accumulated institutional knowledge about how criminal and state-level threat actors operate across hidden channels, packaged into a platform that produces investigative outcomes. That is structurally difficult to replicate quickly, and it is exactly the category of intelligence capability that governments with growing cyber budgets are willing to pay for.

The "Palantir of Asia" framing, for all its hubris, captures something real: Palantir's durable competitive position came not from its software architecture but from the operational depth it developed working inside US intelligence agencies over years. S2W is attempting the same accumulation process in Asia, starting with Korea and INTERPOL, extending into Japan and Singapore, and building a reference network of government customers whose endorsement becomes self-reinforcing. The company is earlier in this process than Palantir was when it went public, and the execution risk is real.

But the underlying thesis is sound: dark web intelligence is a market that will grow faster than the broader cybersecurity sector, government buyers in Asia are under-served by existing Western vendors, and S2W has the only peer-reviewed dark web AI model, the only Korean INTERPOL partnership, and a founder team that has demonstrated the ability to win contracts in markets it had no presence in three years ago. For global investors building a Korea exposure, that combination is rare.

Bone AI

Defense robotics  ·  Seed $12M  ·  Seoul + Palo Alto  ·  Founded 2024

Founded by the co-founder of MarqVision, Bone AI combines AI software with hardware manufacturing to build drones, ground unmanned vehicles, and maritime unmanned vessels for defense customers. What makes it notable is the traction velocity: within its first year, it was generating $3M in revenue, landing seven-figure government contracts, and had already completed an acquisition (D-Makers) to internalize hardware IP. The $12M seed round — backed by Third Prime and Kolon Group, with the founder committing $1.5M personally — signals a founder with high conviction and strategic investors who want defense-tech exposure in Korea. Given the backdrop of Korea's defense budget expansion and the government's "ABCDEF" deep tech industrial strategy, Bone is worth watching as one of the first Korean defense-tech startups with cross-Pacific capital structure.

Seed Defense

WIRobotics

Humanoid / exoskeleton robotics  ·  Series B KRW 95B (~$68M)  ·  May 2026

WIRobotics closed a KRW 95B Series B in May 2026 — one of the largest robotics rounds in Korean history — on the back of its ALLEX humanoid platform, which is built around technologies designed to understand and augment human movement. Revenue has been doubling every year: ₩560M in 2023, ₩1.3B in 2024, ₩2.79B in 2025, with Q1 2026 alone already exceeding full-year 2024. The Series B follows a ₩13B Series A just two years prior, suggesting strong investor conviction in the trajectory. The physical AI narrative is global right now — Figure, 1X, Apptronik are all raising at multiples of WIRobotics' implied valuation — and the company's medical/rehabilitation heritage gives it a credible deployment pathway that pure-play humanoid startups lack.

Series B Robotics

Ticaros

CAR-T immunotherapy  ·  Series D KRW 20.8B  ·  Bio/Oncology  ·  2026

Korean biotech has historically struggled to translate academic research into clinical-stage assets that global pharma takes seriously. Ticaros is a notable exception: a CAR-T immunotherapy developer that closed a KRW 20.8B Series D in 2026, putting it at the late-stage end of Korea's oncology pipeline. CAR-T is a high-stakes, high-capital segment — US approvals (Kymriah, Yescarta) have created a global market but existing products target limited indications and carry significant manufacturing complexity. The Korea structural advantage here mirrors S2W's in cyber: hospitals, patient data, and clinical trial infrastructure are highly digitized and nationally coordinated, providing a research velocity advantage that matters in clinical AI and immunotherapy development. Ticaros reaching Series D without a Western acquirer signals either genuine independence ambitions or a pricing disagreement — worth watching to see which.
